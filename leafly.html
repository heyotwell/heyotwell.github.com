Work at Leafly.com

Leafly.com is the "World's Cannabis Information Resource", and is the most popular site in the rapidly-expanding cannabis industry. It gets more than 10 million pageviews monthly to cannabis dispensary listings, strain database, and News content. My role was primarily Product Manager, though I also did the UX design work below (except where I've noted).

Leafly Biz

Leafly Biz is used by thousands of cannabis businesses to maintain their Leafly listings, which includes managing menus (typically with several hundred items), running deals, posting updates to Leafly consumers, and updating their business information.

I led the redesign of the 3-year-old version of this tool using Google's Material Design language with the goal of modernizing and professionalizing this tool, and making improvements to navigation and key tasks such as daily menu management. I was the UX designer on the product, but also wrote bug testing scripts and planned the product launch. Since launch in Fall 2016, I managed the product as a Product Manager, and work with Sales, Marketing, and other teams to build the product roadmap and develop new features. I did bug triaging and prioritization with our development team, and worked with analysts and marketing to track KPIs.

Dispensary Menu Management

Cannabis dispensaries manage huge retail product menus (like this one), often changing them daily. We built menu management in Leafly Biz to allow quick access to updating single or multiple products, and to customize sort order and featured items.

We debated about how to support setting the order of menu items and whether to support bulk menu changes. The old version of the managment tool did allow bulk item deletion, but it was rarely used and since it lacked a way to undo or restore edits, it was too easy to accidently lose data. In fact, we found in research that clients didn't need to delete items often, since the entry would need to be recreated from scratch if that product came back in stock. We made show and hide single-click actions instead. You can see these decisions in these wireframes, where I removed the checkbox selection per row item and added the inline icons for other actions.

Why no drag and drop? We found many clients wanted to make a few specific changes when ordering menu items: put something to the top of the list, group a few items together (perhaps by brand), or to sort everything A-Z. Drag and drop could be a better interaction for moving a few items around, but it was not critical for that task. It also wasn't supported natively in the Material framework we used, and so would have meant an unknown enginneering cost. As Product Manager, I made the call to not try to add drag and drop for these reasons.
